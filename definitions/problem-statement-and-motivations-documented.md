# Problem Statement and Motivations Documented

## What this means
The problem statement captures the core challenge this initiative addresses: what is broken or missing, who is affected, how severe the impact is, and why solving it now is the right priority. It forms the foundation that all design and implementation decisions should trace back to.

## Why it matters
Without a clear problem statement, teams are prone to building solutions to the wrong problem or over-engineering solutions to minor inconveniences. A well-written problem statement acts as a forcing function — any proposed feature or design choice should clearly connect back to the stated problem. It also creates alignment across product, engineering, and leadership before significant investment is made.

## How to complete this
- Start from observed user behavior or data, not from a preconceived solution
- Quantify the problem where possible: "X% of users abandon at step Y" is stronger than "the flow is confusing"
- Include the root cause, not just the symptom
- Document the "why now" — what changed in the business, market, or technical environment that makes this worth solving today
- Have the problem statement reviewed by someone outside the immediate team to ensure it's clear and compelling

## Definition of done
- [ ] Problem statement is written and posted in the project doc or checklist item
- [ ] The statement is quantified with data where available
- [ ] Root cause, not just symptoms, is addressed
- [ ] "Why now" is articulated
- [ ] Reviewed and approved by PM and engineering lead
