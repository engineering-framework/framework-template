# User Stories and Acceptance Criteria Written

## What this means
User stories translate requirements into the language of user needs, expressing functionality from the perspective of who will use it and why. Acceptance criteria define the precise conditions that must be true for a story to be considered complete — removing ambiguity between what was built and what was intended.

## Why it matters
Ambiguous requirements are the leading cause of rework. When acceptance criteria are written before development begins, engineers can make the right trade-offs, QA knows exactly what to test, and the team has a shared understanding of done. Stories without acceptance criteria invariably lead to "that's not what I meant" conversations at the end of a sprint.

## How to complete this
- Write stories in the "As a [user], I want [goal], so that [benefit]" format — be specific about who the user is
- Write acceptance criteria in Given/When/Then (or similar structured) format
- Cover the happy path, error cases, and boundary conditions in the criteria
- Include non-functional requirements in criteria where relevant (e.g., "page loads within 2 seconds")
- Get sign-off from PM, engineering, and QA before development begins
- Keep stories small enough to be completed within a single sprint

## Definition of done
- [ ] All stories in the current scope are written with clear acceptance criteria
- [ ] Each story covers at least one happy path and one error/edge case scenario
- [ ] Stories reviewed and accepted by PM, tech lead, and QA representative
- [ ] No story is larger than can be completed and tested within one sprint
